We show that the rich–lazy crossover in deep tanh networks can be understood as a gain-controlled, non-equilibrium geometric transition in the learned input-space deformation. In the lazy regime, training remains close to the initialization geometry, FTLE ridges are not boundary-anchored, and the correlation between maximal FTLE and adversarial margin is near zero. In the rich regime, training reorganizes the feature map so that maximal stretching localizes at the decision boundary and its principal direction aligns with the boundary normal, driving the FTLE–margin correlation toward −1. As the initialization gain g decreases, the intermediate crossover narrows and becomes increasingly step-like across the ensemble of training runs, consistent with a finite-size-rounded, first-order-like dynamical phase transition between lazy and rich learning.
